Github’s Issue tracking

Redesign Github’s issue tracking experiences

A design exercise to help developers create more structured issues, and keep track of issues that really matter to them and to the project.

Last week, I received a design exercise from Turi team and had about 5 days to work on it. The goal of this exercise is not to design a perfect solution or a perfect tool, because you can’t really tackle a complicated problem in just 5 days with the perfect result. It is to showcase design skills, thinking process, and how I make design decisions.

I will next cover what the challenge is, some background information. I will break it down piece by piece, walking you through research findings, problem solving process, and the final result.

The Challenge

Redesign Github’s Issue tracking experiences

GitHub Issues can be problems, ideas, bugs need to be discussed or solved in a project on Github.

In the current version, users can see a list of issues already being created or create a new issue within a project. Sometimes, they need to read through the long list in order to find issues that matter. And sometimes, issues might be loosely defined that they need extra information to make actionable items.

Think about an issue’s life cycle. Design an experience to
1) help developers create more structured issues, and
2) keep track of issues that really matter to them and to the project.

Background

When I first saw this question, I was actually very confused with the term GitHub Issue and couldn’t comprehend the design goal. Constraints, Scope and Assumptions

Because it is a design exercise with limited time and resources, I set up some rules and constraints to help me scope down and focus on the design goal.

1. I have seen that different team dynamic interact with GitHub Issue differently. I am targeting industry/working environment of team structure today. Use case will be limited for this scope. Team structure is 1 Technology Lead managing about 8 developers.

2. Our two goals, one is to create structured issue so developers can make actionable items. Another is to keep track of Issues. I set the success measurement for goal 1, creating structured issues, is seeing less back and forth conversation showing up about what is the current state, what is the project version, and how to reproduce the bug. For keeping track of issues, I decided that the measurement is to see people working more effectively, which is monitoring the data of Issue’s closing rate.

3. I have found other pain points of issue, for example, multiple things to do under one Issue, and duplication of Issue. However, they are not in scope of our two main goals of design. I decided not to focus on them at this time. 

Note: It is not saying they are not important. I will discuss them in next step section.

Brainstorming Ideas

How might we create structured issue? How might we keep track of issue?

Some of the sticker notes made during brainstorming sessions

I set myself several rounds of brainstorming, each round about 10 to 20 minutes, around how might we create structured issue and how might we keep track of issue. 

After a few iterations, I decided to move forward with idea of Issue Template for filling Issue and categorization of Issue List.

I was hesitating between the ideas of Issue Dash Board and Issue Categorization. I moved forward with the Idea of Issue Categorization because dashboard provides less information on the first sight, and in situation of many opened Issues, dashboard does not provide enough space.

Issues are now categorized based on their status. It is easier to be tracked for both the Technology Lead and the Developers and shows what Issues matter to the project

The Issue Template provides all the critical information needed for developers to make actionable items, and it is also customizable since different project might need different Issue information.

Next Steps

Spend more time on research and go beyond of work environment. Targeted size, no longer just 10, but more collaborators. For example, in open sourced team project that doesn’t have a tech lead/project owner. Understand their issue life cycle, and whether they use label while categorizing issues or not, and why.

Tackling problems such as breaking up an issue, decrease duplication.

If really an actual project in a company, discuss details with pm and other teammates about who are target users really are, and identifying competitors such as Jira and other bug trackers on the market. Tackle the problem whether they are success or not, and why.

See measurements. If we still see measurement of this design solution does not work out, we could go over the process and further understand the root of the problem, because design failure is not a failure. Now you know this route doesn’t work, and we are always prepared to brainstorm more solutions and have plans to test them out.
